# Task III : Open Task

I was first introduced to quantum computing during the ThinkingBeyond Introduction to Quantum Research program, where I was part of the first cohort. During this program we studied the basic structure of quantum computers and the mathematical foundations required to understand them, particularly linear algebra, complex vector spaces, and quantum mechanics.

One of the key ideas that stood out to me early was that quantum computing does not aim to replace classical computing for every task. Instead, it provides advantages for specific classes of problems, particularly those involving large search spaces, high-dimensional linear algebra, and certain structured optimization problems.

Understanding when quantum methods are actually useful, and when classical methods remain superior, is an important aspect of quantum computing research.

## Hands-on Experience

As part of the hands-on component of the program, we used **Qiskit** to build and simulate simple quantum circuits. Through these exercises we explored fundamental quantum operations such as:

- preparing qubits in **superposition**
- applying quantum gates such as **Hadamard, Pauli, and rotation gates**
- observing how **entanglement** can correlate multiple qubits
- performing **measurement** and understanding probabilistic outcomes

For example, one of the first experiments involved simulating a **quantum coin flip** using a Hadamard gate. While this is a simple example, it illustrates how a qubit can represent a superposition of states, and the final measurement outcome depends on how amplitudes evolve through the circuit.

## Team Project

Towards the end of the program, we worked in teams on a small research project and also presented a poster! Our team explored **Quantum Recurrent Neural Networks (QRNNs)** and compared them with classical **Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)** for **carbon footprint forecasting**, a time-series prediction task.

This project required us to first understand classical sequence models such as **RNNs, LSTMs, and time-series forecasting pipelines**.

Our experiments showed that the QRNN achieved **performance comparable to classical RNN models** on the dataset we used.

This result was interesting because it highlighted an important reality in **quantum machine learning**:

> Quantum approaches do not automatically outperform classical ones. In many practical scenarios, especially with small datasets or shallow circuits, classical models remain highly competitive.
